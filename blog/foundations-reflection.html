My reflections on Foundations

14 July, 2023

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

I've learnt that I actually have a lot more transferrable skills to programming than I originally thought. I went into this course thinking that my previous career in the arts wouldn't really help me out at all in this field, but I feel like my time working in a small team really helped me learn good human skills which align well with what we've learnt in Core.

I also learnt which of my values and strengths align well with programming, and how I can use them to improve my code, along with now having self-awareness of my weaknesses and how they can potentially trip me up. I've seen an improvement in my ability to tough it out when things go wrong with my code, and I give up less easily because I'm now remembering to encourage myself. I'm also now remembering to take breaks more frequently now, because I know I can easily get sucked into a problem for too long.

Lastly, learning about neuroplasticity has been really enlightening and has helped me to understand how I can best support my brain as it learns. Alongside trying to code often, I've been playing some brain training games and solving puzzles to keep my brain working in a bunch of different ways.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

I think it's really important during any learning journey to be aware of your own values, particularly if it's going to initiate a career change. Wrestling with code can be really challenging, and there's nothing quite like stress to show you who you really are. During moments of stress and transition, thinking back to your root values and how they define you helps you centre yourself as you find the right path forward.

Empathy fosters our perception of others, and of the world around us. It helps us to improve our attention to detail, whether it's reading how someone is feeling, or where that little mistake is hidden in our code. It's not only a great skill to have if you're working as part of a team, but a really useful way to assess yourself and others during the process of coding and further learning. It also becomes important when pair programming with someone, as you work together to write good quality code.

Self-awareness is vital for evaluating your own learning journey and your growing coding skills. Having a healthy awareness of yourself fosters a growth mindset as you consider how far you've come, and what you can do to improve further - both when it comes to your problem-solving abilities, and in the context of refactoring your code. This awareness and ability to know where you're at makes you a better overall learner and programmer. It's about learning smarter, not harder.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

I think what surprised me most was how much it resonated with a lot of my past explorations of myself, both alone and through guidance. It was reassuring to see that these explorations can help me on my journey to become a programmer.

I also was pleasantly surprised by how much Core actually helped me improve my solo programming skills. I feel like I am much better under stress now when I'm in the thick of problem-solving code, and as a result I sometimes end up coming to a solution more easily because I'm not focused on panicking. I thought Core would mostly be useful when working with others, but it's been really helpful while working alone too.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

I found examining my own values and strengths surprisingly hard. That ended up being the most challenging aspect of Core for me, and it took me a while to write an exploration of my identity and values. While I often research self-improvement, I don't often examine myself quite as deeply as I needed to for that exercise. It was really useful.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

I think it's so much of a focus because programming isn't just about fingers on keyboards, writing code. It's also about how humans understand and working with the code, and how our brains develop and learn what is eseentially a logical language. Learning to code is hard, and focusing on Core makes sense for helping us to examine ourselves ahead of time so we can anticipate what we'll find challenging as we learn. Being prepared for challenge ahead of time can help to strengthen our resolve and prevent complete discouragement about our abilities. It also better prepares us for the job search afterwards, as employers are very much looking for human skills alongside coding ability. Coding is only one half of it - how you get on well with the team is just as important.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

Not at all! In fact, I think it's been a really crucial part of my learning during Foundations. Taking the time to focus on Core each week has helped me remember to check in with myself, which I've found so helpful during all the learning and problem-solving we've been doing.

When I've done self-guided web development courses in the past, they taught me a lot about coding itself, but not as much about how to persevere through the emotional struggles and challenges of problem-solving code. I feel like I've really sharpened my problem-solving abilities just by learning how I react during difficult moments, and how I can temper that to help me push through and find a solution. Learning more about myself in the context of coding feels like it's going to set me up really well for embarking on a career in an industry which is brand new to me - i.e. the tech industry.
