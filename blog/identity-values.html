Identity, Values and Strengths

24 June, 2023

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

In my most recent job, I worked closely with artworks which often had specific limitations imposed on their display in my workplace (an art gallery) to ensure the artworks would not be damaged overtime by the surrounding environment. Last year, we invited in two guest curators to select artworks to be displayed in an exhibition, and I was responsible for coordinating and overseeing the exhibition installation.

During this installation period, one of the guest curators insisted that we needed to brighten the lights above some old photographic artworks which had never been displayed in public before, and which had very specific maximum light levels which were safe for them to be exposed to. He thought that the room was too dark. My manager asked me to follow the guest curator's wishes and raise the light level on the artworks. Knowing that going above the max light levels could cause permanent damage to those artworks, I immediately went back to the loan agreement to check the limits on these artworks only to find that this was ambiguous; the lending institution had accidentally put two different max light level values into the agreement, and we didn't know which was correct.

It was difficult to balance respecting the wishes of my manager and the guest curator, while also ensuring that I did not put the artworks in an unsafe situation. I asked that we hold off on raising the light levels until we had clarification from the lending institution on what the exact max safe light level was. There was a large time difference which delayed their response, so I had to be polite but firm on waiting and focusing on a different aspect of the installation until we had clarity. The guest curator and my manager felt that raising the lights briefly wouldn't be harmful, but I did not know this for certain and I didn't want to risk it when we could easily wait for clarification and had time to do so.

In retrospect, I am glad that I stuck to my values and did what I felt was right for the artworks. It wasn't easy to speak up against my manager and the guest curator, but ultimately we were being trusted by the lending insitution to care for these highly valuable and fragile artworks and I did not want to break that trust. In a gallery environment, staff protect people first, and then artworks; since no people were in danger in this situation, prioritising the artworks felt like the right thing to do and was my responsibility as a gallery staff member.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

My mum has always been a good example to me about persisting through challenges with unapologetic indepencence and drive. A lot of my ambition and inner strength is modelled off her. I think I have always been open to other people's life experiences, but this open mindedness has definitely been strengthened by all the people I've befriended over the years. A lot of my past and current friends come from very different cultural and socioeconomic backgrounds to me. I have always been open and curious about their journeys and willing to listen and understand their backgrounds.

A big value of mine is compassion, which I think stems from the shyness and anxiety I experienced as a child. I remember a lot of the fears and difficulties I had and want to help others who have had similar experiences. Tutoring in University showed me that I love to help and guide people through things, whether academic or personal.

As a child I was homeschooled for a decent chunk of my education, and was able to pursue whatever academic interests I had. I loved to explore the world and people's stories through reading, and really enjoyed history and art. This time in my life really nutured my enthusiasm for creativity and my imagination, and introduced me to the concept of learning at my own pace.

When I was young I was shy, anxious and a real people pleaser, always worried about upsetting others to the extent that I could be self-sacrificing. As I grew up and experienced being taken advantage of, I gradually became more assertive and began to stand up for myself. It's really important to me to be in control of my own journey and identity, and to speak up when I or others are being wronged.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

  • Persistence, patience: This will help me work through troubleshooting issues with my code, or other technical challenges I might face
  • Curiosity, eagerness to learn: This will be valuable in helping me learn unfamiliar course content and research it further
  • Openness, creativity: This allows me to think of different solutions/strategies to try when I am coding something
  • Friendliness, personable nature: This can help me to collaborate with others

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

I can be quick to frustration if something goes wrong. I need to remember to step back from a problem and give myself a break when I'm not making headway.

I can get anxious, withdrawn and worry that I won't figure something out, and then let that worry cloud my ability to problem solve. I need to remember to trust the process and ask others for help when I'm really stuck.

When I'm researching ways to fix/improve my code, often I over-complicate it and try to do something that is too overboard for the issue I'm having. I need to try and pull back from getting deep into an overly complex path, and try to see if there's a simpler way to resolve an issue first.

I can be quite hard on myself and it can affect my self-confidence, especially in an unfamiliar sector like this one. A big part of why I didn't try out development sooner was because I thought I was too 'artsy' and creative for the IT/Tech sector. If I give in to the voice in my head which tries to discourage me and put me into a box, I can risk not taking opportunities I'd actually do really well in. It's also important for me to remember that it's okay to fail, and that failure is actually an opportunitiy for growth.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

Last year there was a conflict which happened in my shared flat, where all of the flatmates disagreed about the best/safest practices if one of us fell sick with covid. I ended up taking on a mediator role between my other flatmates, and encouraged them both to share their concerns and opinions. I wrote down what they both wanted, and then tried to find a middle ground we were all comfortable with. It worked well for some things we were concerned about, but there were still some things we just couldn't all agree on and we kind of had to leave those things to one side.

I think in retrospect, it would have been more effective for me to assert myself and add my voice to the conversation more instead of mainly just listening and mediating. I think if I had added in more of my own opinions based on the current science, that may have helped resolve some of the issues we just weren't agreeing on. I also think it would have been important to recognise when the discussion was no longer productive, and to remind everyone to take a break and cool off a bit before reconvening.
