Finding your emotional intelligence

Looking at how EQ can set us up for success

26 June, 2023

What is emotional intelligence?

Emotional intelligence is all about being aware of your own emotions, and the emotions of others. Those high in emotional intelligence are able to reflect on their feelings mindfully, regulate their emotions and understand where these are stemming from, and respond appropriately.

Some traits of emotionally intelligent people are:

  • The ability to think mindfully and reflectively about their emotions and those of others, improving their ability to relate to others in an effective, constructive way
  • Control and regulation of their own emotions, being understanding of their own and other's emotions, and knowing how to respond
  • Identifying and describing how others are feeling, and knowing their own strengths and limits
  • Accepting their emotions, reactions and those of others, with curiosity rather than judgement
  • Practising self-compassion, while also having empathy and concern for others
  • Taking responsibility for their mistakes and learning from them
  • Being aware of and managing their emotions in difficult moments
  • Actively listening to others and being present in the moment with them

How is EQ different to IQ?

EQ (Emotional Quotient) accounts for more of your thinking and interpersonal abilities than just a traditional assessment of your intelligence (IQ). IQ tests tend to focus on your ability to logically problem solve, learn, strategise, understand abstract ideas, and your applied use of language. This is very much a traditional and academic model of assessing your abilities.

EQ takes this further and considers your emotional intelligence about yourself and others, including abilities such as identifying and reflecting on your emotions and those of others, empathising with others, adapting your behaviour and feelings to different situations, impulse control and emotional regulation, conflict resolution and effective communication including active listening, and being fully, mindfully present in the moment with others.

In both IQ and EQ assessments, your ability to problem solve can be tested. The key difference is that in IQ tests you'd be problem solving something more technical or logical, such as a mathematics problem, whereas in EQ tests you'd be assessed on your ability to problem solve an interpersonal issue such as a conflict between yourself and another person.

Why is EQ important?

When you work at a company, you're usually always working as part of a team of people. The overall productivity and success of the company depends on everyone getting along, working effectively together, communicating well, and handling conflict resolution in an appropriate and professional manner. Having a high EQ demonstrates that you'll be able to work well within a company's existing team, adapt to the interpersonal environment of the office and therefore produce effective, good quality work in collaboration with your coworkers.

Not only does having a high EQ improve your professional life, it can have a hugely beneficial impact on your personal life too. Emotional intelligent people tend to experience better mental health, have more satisfying relationships with others, and cope better when faced with challenging life situations. Emotional intelligence can also improve your ability to mediate arguments and improve your overall self-confidence.

What are some ways to improve your emotional intelligence?

To know and accept others, it's important to understand and accept yourself. Practising mindfulness through a regular meditation habit is a great way to check in with yourself. Mindfulness involves actively recognising and accepting any thoughts or feelings, no matter how challenging or uncomfortable they may be, and sitting with these quietly.

Practising actively listening to others nutures your ability to empathise, relate to, and commmunicate mindfully with your peers. Sitting and listening to a friend, colleague or loved one speak for a while without being poised to reply to their opinions and thoughts, can give you a greater appreciation for their perspective and a better overall understanding of who they are and where they are coming from. It also becomes an important skill when resolving interpersonal conflict.

Allowing yourself to feel your emotions when they surface is an important step in becoming better at self-regulation. When we push down our negative emotions and bottle them up, they rise again sooner or later and are usually stronger than before. This can lead to greater pain than if we had let them out in the first place. Identifying your present emotion, letting it wash over you and venting/crying/writing about how it makes you feel and the thoughts which come up is a very cathartic experience. You're likely to feel much better afterwards.

To sum up

Emotional intelligence is not a skill we are born with, but an important one which we can foster over the course of our lives. Understanding your own emotions and those of others is not just a great skill to help you succeed in the workplace, but a valuable tool to help you weather all the personal and professional challenges of life - while also giving you a greater appreciation for all the joys.
