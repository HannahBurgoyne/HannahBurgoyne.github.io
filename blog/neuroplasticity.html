Neuroplasticity and Growth

Finding the right frame of mind for success

03 July, 2023

How can understanding neuroplasticity benefit us?

Knowing how your mind works can be really empowering - everyone at some stage in their life has felt like they just aren't capable of understanding something. Being aware that your brain can make new connections, and can develop over time in response to what you feed it, can empower you with the confidence to push through the challenges of learning a new skill. Understanding exactly how the brain makes these new connections, and how we can support our brains to become plastic more easily, allows us to make the most of our learning opportunities and learn effectively. Researching neuroplasticity allows us to build a gameplan of sorts for our learning, ensuring that our brain is stimulated in ways which work for us, gets regular consistent practice at the skill we're learning, and recieves good quality rest and proper nutrition.

By being aware of your brain's potential to develop, you can help to reframe how you percieve yourself. If you've ever felt like you put limits on your learning by telling yourself that you don't have the capacity to change, and will always be stuck with the brain and knowledge you have, then knowing that your brain is malleable can inspire you to keep learning a new skill even through the toughest challenges.

How can you engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit?

Personally, I will try to give my brain the best environment to plasticise! I'll prioritise getting a good sleep each night, getting regular exercise and eating nutritional foods to support my focus and learning. I'll also keep up a regular coding practice to keep working the new connections in my brain and solidify my learning by putting it into practice.

Knowing that my brain has the ability to change and improve over time is something I'll remind myself of when I face challenges during my learning. It's encouraging to know that getting stuck, or failing, is an opportunity to further my learning by understanding what went wrong and how to resolve it in the future. It's good to know that challenging myself creates an environment where my brain can focus in and learn better. I'm also very interested in the concept of visualising as a form of practice - I think I can apply this to my own learning by imagining what it would be like to already have a career in the tech industry, and what that could look like for me. This will set me up for success once I graduate this course and enter the industry for the first time.

How can you increase your neuroplasticity?

Brain games are a good way to stimulate your mind and challenge it in new and diverse ways. Teasers, crosswords, sudokus, and more are all useful for improving executive function, and improving memory and processing speed.

Learning something new is a great way to build new connections in your brain. It can be coding, or playing an instrument, or learning a new language. You can also expand beyond these to throw yourself into new experiences - find a new social group, go travelling, try creating some art, read a new book, get imaginative.

Mindfulness is a really useful tool for sharpening your focus and present awareness as you learn a new skill. It can help train your brain to stay on task and take in the content you are consuming.

Physical wellness, such as getting enough good quality REM sleep, eating a good diet, and exercising regularly help your brain to cement its learning and supports it as it works in new ways. Sleep in particular solidifies your progress and boosts your brain's performance, giving it the space to recharge and retain your new knowledge.

Reduce overall stress to help support your brain as it makes new connections. Stress can weaken brain-derived neurotropic factor (BDNF), which is responsible for growing new connections and inter-neuron signalling. By reducing and managing your stress, you can keep levels of BDNF in your brain high enough to keep boosting its neuroplasticity.

What is a growth mindset and why is it relevant?

A growth mindset is the belief that you can change core aspects of yourself over time. This includes your personality, your intellectual abilities, and your self-beliefs. It means you strive to learn, adapt and change through challenges and obstacles, and that you see yourself as having the potential to improve beyond your current state of being.

Having a growth mindset is in opposition to the fixed mindset. In a fixed mindset, people have the self-limiting belief that they were born with one personality and set of skills which they cannot change. Because they do not believe they are able to improve, they do not challenge themselves to do better, and end up stuck and unable to adapt positively to life's challenges. Realising that you can move beyond your current sense of self can be hugely motivational in improving aspects of your life which you aren't satisfied with.

Cultivating a growth mindset is relevant and important because it builds resilience in the face of challenge. As we take the risk of learning a new skill, including changing our lifestyle and our circumstances, believing that we can persevere and improve at this new skill keeps us confident in our abilities and our capacity to improve even when we suffer setbacks. A growth mindset fosters emotional resilience and fuels an inner drive to keep going, even when the going gets tough.

Did anything surprise you or change for you as you explored this topic?

I went into this exercise having some familiarity with a growth mindset, but this exploration really helped me see how it relates to my own life experience. Until my mid-20s I was pretty convinced that I was only able to be good at creative and arts/humanities subjects, and my struggles in STEM subjects during high school reinforced my beliefs that my brain couldn't be good at anything outside the arts realm. I didn't realise at the time how much I was limiting myself with this mindset.

Learning about the growth mindset has encouraged me to pursue other things I'm interested in outside of the arts, such as coding and other technicial areas. These are things I've always had an interest in, but never thought I could actually do well at. Knowing that I can adapt and improve has given me the confidence to actually challenge myself in these areas and to not be afraid of failure.

This exploration has also made me reflect on my attitude to failure. I was terrified of failing when I was in school, and the way academic work is graded reinforced my belief that failure is a negative thing. It encouraged me to stay in my comfort zone and not take risks. Coming to study at Dev Academy has in itself been a big leap of faith and really challenged me in my fear. I'm now beginning to reframe failure as an opportunity to learn, rather than reinforcing the idea that I can't code.

How can I integrate a growth mindset into my learning journey?

I plan to reframe my attitude to failure by seeing mistakes and problems as opportunities to learn what went wrong, and how I can grow my knowledge and understanding from this obstacle. As part of this, I'll exercise awareness about how I talk to myself when problems arise - encouraging myself to push through, rather than accepting defeat and thinking that I'm not cut out for this.

I also plan to consciously encourage my brain's neuroplasticity to support my growth. I'll try to stimulate my brain through challenges, both in coding and in the form of puzzles and teasers, and through getting regular rest and maintaining a healthy lifestyle. I'll also throw myself out of my comfort zone more and remind myself that challenges will lead to further growth and development.

How can my understandings around neuroplasticity and a growth mindset shape my learning plan for Dev Academy?

My current learning plan incorporates daily exercise, so I will continue to keep that up. I will ensure I take breaks when I need them and spent some time on mindfulness daily to help my brain relax into its new learning. I will try to keep up coding daily to grow my understanding and confidence of the new material I'm learning. When I get stuck on problems I'll keep returning to them and trying to figure them out.

I'll also try some visualisation to support my practice, particularly trying to visualise key coding concepts. I think that could be a really interesting way to support my current learning.
